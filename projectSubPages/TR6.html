4. ​​Intake and Exhaust Manifolds
As part of the general refresh i decided it would be of benefit to my future self to replace intake and exhaust manifold studs. After taking the manifolds off the studs as expected were gummed up and stuck (4a). After cleaning the mating surface with a plastic razor blade, I fit the new studs and gasket (4b). Later cleaned up the manifolds with degreaser and a brass wire brush.

5. ​​​Zinc Plating Hardware
As I got deep into tearing the car down, I quickly realized all of the hardware on this fine automobile are fine threaded imperial bolts... As this hardware is not easily accessible at hardware stores, I decided to save the cost and reuse the existing hardware.

To have a bit of fun and ease the worry of reusing rusty bolts, I threw together a quick zinc electroplating solution with materials available at local stores. One of the first bolts being plated can be seen in (5a). Other plated parts can be seen in 5b & 5c, the oil drain plug and brake line flared nut respectively. 

6. Distributor and Ignition Coil
01/28/25 - After a failed start attempt, I realized I was a tad bit naieve to think a car sitting for 20 years would start on the old ignition system. So, here is me refreshing the distributor and replacing the ignition coil.

Took this picture of the car with the original white wall tires on, man it looks good (6a). Began by removing the old distributor (6b) the points along with everything else were covered in rust or dirt. Got the new ignition coil, plug wires, and distributor parts in and assembled (6c).

Finally, after resetting the engine timing and getting the distributor back in I thought it was again going to start. Video (6d) shows the failed start attempt, recorded at ~6am. This was one of many all nighters pulled to get this car running.  

7. Brake and Clutch Master Cylinder
Need a win after the multiple failed start attempts, so I took 2 days to rebuild the brake master and clutch cylinder. The master cylinder is shown in part 2 of the series, in not great condition (hyperlink)... The original slave cylinder is shown in (7a), the aluminum push rod corroded, boot ripped, etc. Because of this damage, I figured it was best to replace the unit.

After reading many forums online, I decided to attempt a rebuild of the original brake master cylinder. After cleaning and electroplating the original casting, there was still major pitting in the bore (7b). Hindsight is 20-20, but I should quit while I was ahead and bought a new unit. However, i still went about cleaning the original parts (7c) (to the best of my ability) and reassembling the unit (7d).

Skipping ahead in time a bit, the rebuilt cylinder would not hold pressure and I could not bleed the brakes. I purchased a new unit, and switched the fluid reservoir to keep the original patina (7e). Got the new clutch cylinder in and bent the tube to the slave (7f). Unfortunately, I did not capture and photos of bending the brake lines, however new front brake lines were cut, bent, flared, and installed. The rear lines were cleaned and pressure tested. Finally the hydraulics in the car were complete, and all systems bled (7g).

11. Electric Fuel Pump
2/5/2025 To get the car running under its own power, the last thing to do was install a new fuel pump (along with fixing a few minor electrical issues).

The car comes with a mechanical fuel pump, driven off of a cam internal to the engine. It's known to be unreliable, so I figured installing an electric fuel pump would be wise. ​Riveted the diaphragm pump to a flat on the wheel well (11a), added a relay and added a fuse to the empty terminal in the fuse box to supply power on ignition.

Once installed, I was finally able to drive the car into its resting spot, instead of pushing it uphill as I had for the past month (11b).

12. Vacuum Lines
2/7/2025 - Getting to the final touches of a fully driving vehicle, it was time to replace all of the vacuum lines. Silicone hoses are both cheaper and superior to the original rubber lines, specifically in longevity with greater chemical resistance.  

The reality of my workspace at times shown in (12a). All of the work outlined was typically done at night, after a full days of class and work, so here is me at 2am looking jolly as ever (12b). Replacing all of the lines was a bit tedious, but overall an easy win (12c)

13. Coolant Flush
2/7/2025 - The final task before taking the TR6 out onto the streets was getting coolant in the engine. Thankfully, the radiator held fluid. Flushing the block with hose water got rid of the crystallized coolant (13a). After getting new coolant in, gave the car a quick wash before the maiden voyage (13b).

Two quick videos, first of which is my documentation of staying up all night and hopefully not foreshadowing disaster (13c). Second is proof of the car getting me home to my apartment complex (13d).
